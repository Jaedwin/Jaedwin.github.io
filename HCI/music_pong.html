Music Pong

by Olga Chitsvarina and Jaedwin Montal

We present a new type of musical instrument. This instrument allows a person with any musical background to make use of physical input to control non-physical output (music). In order to encourage expressive musical performance, we combined two types of physical and tangible input techniques.

The first input is a joystick, which controls musical notes. The performer moves the joystick along the X and Y-axis. Every joystick direction determines the selection of a musical note. We introduced the following mapping between cardinal and intercardinal directions and note sounds: S - C1, SW - D1, W - E1, NW - F1, N - G1, NE - A1, E - B1, and SE - C2. If the joystick is resting, the instrument is soundless.

The second type of input technique involves reading data from photoresistors. Someone performing the instrument places a ball inside the cup. Ball blocks the light. The photoresistor detects this change. The octave changes, depending on the number of cups that hold a ball. If the performer adds a ball, then the octave value increases. If the performer removes the ball, then the octave value decreases. If none of the cups has a ball, then there is no sound coming from the instrument.

Our musical instrument idea was inspired by games played by university students, such as “Near Pong.” It can also be considered as an improvement over the discussed game interface, as players would also be able to utilize musical accompaniment while playing. According to the “Near Pong” game rules, the team that fills all of the opposing team’s cups with balls is the winner. In case all six cups hold a ball, the instrument plays a song leveraging different notes and octaves - a creative surprise to amaze the audience and indicate the end of the game.
