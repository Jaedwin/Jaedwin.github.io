Project Component #1: User Research

Project Idea

Our project idea is the redesign a system we built in our database class. The purpose of the system is an all in one fitness progression tracking application. The application is meant to work in tandem with our database so that a user can create meal plans and add foods to these meal plans based on their macro nutritional/calorie goals. In addition to this, the application also allows the users to create workout routines, and keep track of the weights they use or the number of repetitions achieved for that workout.

We expect our system to be used in a number of different ways. An important functionality for our system is the ability to create meal plans and track food consumption. The average user would be able to input their statistics (height, weight, etc) as well as their goals, and receive the information they require to reach their goals. This can include things like daily calorie limits or macro nutrient goals. In addition to this, users will be able to create workout routines as well as log their weight/repetitions as they complete each exercise in their routine. This allows our users to visually see their progression over time. Lastly, users should be able to do lookups into both the food and exercise database. A user could search the foods database to find new foods that fit their needs, or to log food they consume throughout the day. Similarly, a user could use the exercise database to search up new exercise by body part or by the equipment they have available. This allows users to have greater flexibility with their routines.

Our typical user is meant to be anyone with a phone or computer who wishes to either track their progress, or plan ahead with regards to fitness and health. The only knowledge required to use the application is basic knowledge of phone and web page navigation.

Our target context is ideally both mobile and web-based. The app’s availability on mobile means that users can track on the go, specially since most of the people have smartphones nowadays. The ability to look up macronutrients whilst deciding on what to eat as well as tracking these is a huge selling point, and the same goes for the workout aspect of the app. Having our app available as a website introduces continuity. Users would be able to view their progression on a much larger screen, with a more detailed information provided. This would also allow users to be able to create meal plans and workout routines much more efficiently.

Stakeholders

There are 3 main categories for the training side of the application consisting of beginner, intermediate, and advanced. The beginner user is a user who knows very little of workout routines, such that they can be recommended a routine based on a quick questionnaire. The intermediate users are users who are familiar with a variety of different routines, thus are comfortable with updating and adjusting their routines to suit their needs. Advanced users are users who do not rely on specific routines, rather create routines manually to adjust for their own needs.

As well, there are 3 main categories for the nutrition application side consisting of beginner, intermediate, and advanced. The beginner user is a user who knows very little about nutrition, such that they can be recommended a nutritional plan based on a quick questionnaire. The intermediate users are users who are familiar with nutrition, thus are comfortable with updating and adjusting their specific plans to suit their needs. Advanced users are users who do not rely on specific nutrition plans, rather create plans based on their macronutrients.

Finally, there is an "admin" user. Admin users do not require knowledge of the training side or nutritional side of the application. The admin user's purpose is only execute modifications of the database.

Research Methods

Method #1: Fly on the Wall

Our first method of choice was the fly on the wall observation. We chose fly on the wall because it was easy to execute in a short period of time, and it gives us better view of what goes on at the gym that we don’t notice when we go in for our daily workouts. For the best possible result, we went on three different days, during peak hours. We have narrowed our observations down to the following:

Even though we live in a technological age, we observed some individuals still using a journal or a piece of paper to track their exercises. Most of the fitness coach as well at the university’s fitness centre carry clipboards while training individuals. This observation stood out on people who compete as they can make changes to their exercises and numbers easily depending on how their workout is going. A few left spaces in their journals for comments about their workout. These comments are mainly used to give the person an idea about the future week’s workout. Some of the comments included; (but are not limited to) “Weight felt heavy but doable, stick to the same weight next week”, “Lack of sleep last night, felt weak/tired for the duration of the workout.”

Secondly, during our observation the most prominent trend we witnessed at the gym was that gym goers more often than not, are on their phones between sets of exercise. Upon further inspection we learned that this was for a number reasons. The first of these reasons was to look up exercise routines. The featured picture is a reenactment of our observation. Typically newer gym members could be found on their phones searching up routines. This could be because they either wished for some direction in their workout, or they could not pull from memory the next exercise in their prescribed routine.

Another reason for gym-goers being on their phones we witnessed was to lookup exercises or exercise alternatives. The mobile application ‘progression’ was seen being used by gym goers to perform lookups for alternative exercises. For instance, having used the app ourselves, the user can simply search the database for ‘Chest’ exercises and they are given a veritable smorgasbord of alternate exercises for them to utilize in their routine. Similarly, the application can be used to perform a search by equipment so a user can plan their routine accordingly based on the equipment available. As a bonus, each exercise in the application also has a diagram on how to perform the exercise.

Gym goers were also found using their phones to watch videos. Specifically, videos on proper exercise form. A problem many gym goers face is ensuring they are using proper form when performing a movement. Proper form is one of the most important things regarding weight lifting when it comes to safety. Improper form can lead to poor performance, slow progression, and ultimately injury. In addition, not many gym goers can afford or have access to a trainer to ensure that they are performing an exercise correctly. Thus, the nearest and cheapest available alternative is to utilize information available online.

Research Conclusions

We chose Fly on the Wall and Survey as our methods of observation because we felt the type of data we collected from each method contrast and compliment each other. Survey served as a way to collect general ideas from a larger sample size of people from varying background with somewhat of a bias towards being gym goers. From our surveys we were able to observe general trends from our respondents. As well, we learned how our prospective users were currently meeting their needs, what they liked about their current method, and what they disliked about their current method. Lastly, we gained a general idea on what type of information regarding training and nutrition our respondents actually cared about. Fly on the wall served as a way for us to observe first hand how a smaller sample size of people went about their time at the gym and the nuances of how they train. From personal observation we saw first hand the methods our prospective users were using to meet their needs as well as how these methods affected their training. In addition to this, in watching how people used their phones while they train, we deepened our understanding on what features our users may want in an all in one fitness solution.

During our observations we quickly learned the pros and cons of our chosen methods. Fly on the wall helped give us a broader demographic to learn from because gym goers can range from complete beginners to competitive power lifters. This aided in removing any biases or trends prevalent in a given demographic. However, we encountered a few problems. First, in general most people were on their phone but it was difficult ot discern what exactly they were doing without getting uncomfortably close. In addition to this, not many gym goers are very keen on having candid pictures taken of them for research purposes. In the future we could consider asking the gym goers what they were doing as well since many of our assumptions were made from visual assumptions alone. Survey allowed us to ask a relatively large sample size the questions we thought were relevant to our research. From our serveys we effectively analyzed trends amongst our propsective users. However, because of the strict format of a survey, there is a possibility that we may have overlooked certain aspects. In the future we feel that questions involving spectrums of answers (eg: strongly disagree -- strongly agree) would be more condusive to gaining meaningful feedback from our respondents.

From our research we made the following observations. Gym goers spend a substantial amount of their time on their phones. In addition to this, the tasks the do on their phones are incredibly varied. As well, we observe that the majority of gym goers are also mindful if not at least aware of their food intake. With regards to the different aspects nutrition, our survey respondents had an evenly distribution between which aspect they felt was the most important. Further more, with regards to the different aspects of training, our survey respondants had a similarly even distribution between which aspects they preferred to use as a measurement of their progress. Lastly, from asking our survey respondents as well as personally observing our prospective users, we came to see that there is a plethora of different applications that individual gym goers use to meet their own specific needs, and it is common that each individual requires more than one application. Thus we can conclude that the needs of the market are incredibly diverse and consequently, a fragmented market of fitness applications. This leads us to believe that there is in fact a need for an all in one fitness solution application.

6 Tasks

Create / Modify Meal Plans
Users should be able to create their own meal plans, as well as lookup premade meal plans and be able to use them and modify as needed.
Add / Delete food
Users should have access to a database of different food items with the appropriate nutritional information, which they can track and add to their daily intake. As an error prevention, the option to delete food to the list should be available as well
Create / Modify Workout Routine
Users should be able to create their own workout routine, as well as lookup premade routines and be able to use them and modify as needed
Add / Delete Exercises
Users should have access to a database of different exercises with a clear description of how it is done properly as well as what muscle it tackles. Users are able to add exercises to their routines with the option of adding the number of sets and repetitions as well. As an error prevention, the option to delete an exercise should be available as well
View Graphs to See Overall Progression Within a Certain Time Period
Graphs and charts containing the users progress information should be available to the user. An example would be, users progression on weight for different exercises, average caloric intake which is then split into macro nutrients, etc.
Add Pictures / Weigh Ins
The ability to add weekly/monthly pictures with the option to add weigh in information and comments should be an option for the app. This is for users who are focusing on their physique.
